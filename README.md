Behind the masks: rap album covers

Chromakopia (Tyler, the Creator) 2024 COLOR #C9C7AE
Tyler Okonma set about building an image referencing Alfred Hitchcock and 1940’s Hollywood. To add a sense of the uncanny, a specially-commissioned ceramic mask was made that has described as “transcending” the usual expectations of the aesthetic.

Dark Lane Demo Tapes (Drake) 2020 COLOR #717C16
Drake in a stark black-and-white close-up, partially obscured by shadows, giving the impression of a hidden or fragmented identity. His expression is unreadable, almost masked by darkness rather than a physical object.

I Lay Down My Life for You (JPEGMAFIA) 2024 COLOR #010101
Like much of Peggy's visual work, the mask isn’t just disguise. It becomes a statement on identity, control, and the distortion of self in the digital age. The imagery blurs the line between vulnerability and aggression, echoing the album’s intense, genre-defying sound.

The College Dropout (Kanye West) 2004 COLOR #741314
Kanye West had already taken pictures dressed as the Dropout Bear which would reappear in his later works (Late Registration, Graduation). While the original release of the album features a brown background on the cover artwork, later remasters of the album feature a white background.

Enter The 36 Chambers (Wu-Tang Clan) 1993 COLOR #EECF12
The cover captures the crew inside an abandoned synagogue, all masked in black stocking masks like they’re gearing up for a high-stakes bank robbery. With the now-iconic Wu-Tang “W” stamped across the cover boosted the Clan’s mystique and cemented the album’s legendary aura.

Madvillainy (Madvillian, MF DOOM) 2004 COLOR #F65716
Who was that masked man? This classic black-and-white portrait by Eric Coleman became the most familiar image of underground hip-hop’s most mysterious figure. The ever-elusive MF Doom initially groaned at seeing his face on an album cover, but the image would end up becoming essential to his myth.
